Electrical Kitchen Appliances & Conservation of Energy
Level: BASIC
Topic Conservation of energy in electrical kitchen appliances — oven, refrigerator and dishwasher.
Learning outcomes
  • State the Law of Conservation of Energy and identify the dominant energy transformation in each appliance.
  • Use the formulas P = V × I and E = P × t to calculate power and energy.
  • Distinguish resistive loads (cos φ = 1) from motor/compressor loads (cos φ < 1).
  • Calculate the cost of running an appliance for a given time at a given electricity tariff.
Time needed 60 min
Resources needed Calculator, electricity tariff (e.g. 0.30 €/kWh), appliance nameplate data (or manufacturer website).

Conservation of Energy & Electricity

Conservation of Energy

The Law of Conservation of Energy states that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed. In kitchen appliances:

  • Electrical → Heat (oven, dishwasher)
  • Electrical → Mechanical → Cooling (refrigerator)

Basic Electrical Concepts

  • Power (P): Watt (W)
  • Energy (E): kilowatt-hour (kWh)
  • Voltage (V): Volt
  • Current (I): Ampere
$$ P = V \times I \qquad E = P \times t $$

Power in AC Circuits

$$ P = V \times I \times \cos\varphi $$
  • cos φ = 1 → resistive loads (oven heater)
  • cos φ < 1 → motors and compressors (refrigerator, dishwasher)

1 — Electric Oven

How It Works

An electric oven converts electrical energy into heat using heating elements.

  • Electric current flows through resistors.
  • Heat is generated (Joule effect).
  • Heat cooks food evenly.

Physical Explanation

  • Heat transfer: conduction + convection + radiation.
  • High temperature (up to 250 °C).
  • Closed insulated system.

Structure

  • Heating elements
  • Fan (in some ovens)
  • Thermostat
  • Insulation

Controls

  • Temperature control
  • Timer
  • Cooking modes

Energy Consumption

  • Power: 2.5 kW
  • 1 hour → 2.5 kWh

The electricity consumption is calculated with:

$$ E = P \times t $$
E = energy consumption (kWh) P = power (kW) t = time (hours)

Worked example — 2.5 kW oven for 1 hour

$$ E = 2.5 \times 1 = 2.5 \text{ kWh} $$

If electricity costs 0.30 €/kWh:

$$ \text{Cost} = 2.5 \times 0.30 = 0.75 \text{ €} $$

📊 Result

A 2.5 kW oven running for 1 hour costs approximately 0.75 € (75 euro cents) in electricity.

2 — Refrigerator

How It Works

A refrigerator removes heat from inside and releases it outside.

  • A compressor circulates refrigerant.
  • Heat is absorbed inside the cabinet.
  • Heat is released outside (through the back coils).

Physical Explanation

  • Uses a thermodynamic cycle.
  • Phase change (liquid ↔ gas).
  • Continuous operation.

Structure

  • Compressor
  • Condenser
  • Evaporator
  • Expansion valve

Energy Consumption

  • Power: 0.2 kW
  • 24 hours → 4.8 kWh (running continuously)

3 — Dishwasher

How It Works

A dishwasher cleans dishes using hot water and mechanical action.

  • Water is heated.
  • A pump sprays the water.
  • Detergent removes dirt.

Physical Explanation

  • Heat improves the cleaning action.
  • Water pressure removes food residue.
  • A combination of thermal + mechanical energy.

Structure

  • Heating element
  • Pump motor
  • Spray arms
  • Filters

Controls

  • Program selection
  • Timer
  • Temperature control

Energy Consumption

  • Power: 1.5 kW
  • 1 hour → 1.5 kWh

Worked example — 1.5 kW dishwasher for 1 hour

$$ E = 1.5 \times 1 = 1.5 \text{ kWh} $$

If electricity costs 0.30 €/kWh:

$$ \text{Cost} = 1.5 \times 0.30 = 0.45 \text{ €} $$

📊 Result

A 1.5 kW dishwasher running for 1 hour costs approximately 0.45 € (45 euro cents) in electricity.
