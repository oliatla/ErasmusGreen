Worksheet 17

Sensors, dimming and energy savings
Level: MEDIUM
Topic Sensors, dimming and energy savings
Learning outcomes
  • Students will quantify energy savings achievable with motion sensors and daylight dimming.
  • Students will compare smart lighting payback periods across different room types.
  • Students will recognise the limitations of smart lighting.
Time needed 90 min
Resources needed Calculator; this worksheet.

Introduction

Smart lighting can be very effective in some spaces and almost useless in others. The key is matching the technology to how the room is actually used. In this worksheet you will calculate the energy savings of smart lighting in three different spaces.

What you should already know: You should already know what motion sensors and daylight sensors do (see Worksheet 16), and be confident calculating annual kWh and cost with Energy = Power × time.

Theory

Smart lighting saves energy by reducing "lights on" hours when the room is empty (motion sensors) or when there is enough daylight (daylight dimming). The QUANTITATIVE saving depends entirely on how the room is used.

MOTION SENSORS work best in spaces with LOW occupancy ratio. The occupancy ratio is the share of "lights on" hours that are actually needed. A storage room with 4 hours of lighting but only 0.5 hours of occupancy has a ratio of 12.5 % — meaning 87.5 % potential saving. A classroom with 8 hours of lighting and 6 hours of occupancy has a ratio of 75 % — only 25 % potential saving.

TIME-OUT DELAY: motion sensors don't immediately switch off when the last movement stops. There is a delay (typically 3–15 minutes) so that the light doesn't flicker off if you sit still. This means real motion-sensor savings are slightly less than the theoretical maximum. A common rule of thumb: real "lights on" time ≈ 1.2 × occupied time.

DAYLIGHT DIMMING: a light fixture with a daylight sensor doesn't necessarily switch off completely — it dims to maintain a target lux level. On average over a school year, a daylight-dimmed fixture in a classroom uses around 30 % less energy than the same fixture without dimming, depending on window size and orientation.

PAYBACK: a motion sensor costs €30–€60 installed. A daylight dimmer costs €100–€150 installed. Whether either pays back depends on the room's annual lighting energy use.

Worked example — School toilet

A school toilet has 6 LED tubes of 18 W. Lights are currently on 10 h/day for 190 school days. Toilet is occupied about 2 h/day. A €40 motion sensor is installed.

  1. Step 1: Current annual kWh: (6 × 18 / 1000) × 10 × 190 = 205.2 kWh.
  2. Step 2: With sensor: lights on ≈ 1.2 × 2 = 2.4 h/day. Annual kWh = (6 × 18 / 1000) × 2.4 × 190 = 49.2 kWh.
  3. Step 3: Saving: 205.2 − 49.2 = 156 kWh/year.
  4. Step 4: At €0.25/kWh: €39 saved per year.
  5. Step 5: Simple payback: 40 / 39 ≈ 1.0 year. Investment is highly attractive.

Key terms

TermDefinition
Occupancy ratio Share of "lights on" hours that are actually needed (occupied).
Time-out delay Delay before a motion sensor switches off after the last detected movement (typically 3–15 min).
Daylight dimming Automatically reducing a fixture's power based on available daylight to maintain a target lux level.
Simple payback Installation cost divided by annual saving — how many years until the investment "pays for itself".

Daylight dimming
