Worksheet 12

Floor care — traditional vacuum vs. robot vacuum
Level: ADVANCED
Topic Floor care — traditional vacuum vs. robot vacuum
Learning outcomes
  • Technical knowledge: Students compare energy consumption, cleaning effectiveness, and convenience of different vacuum types.
  • Mathematical skills: Students calculate annual energy costs and lifecycle costs including purchase price and replacement parts.
  • Environmental awareness: Students understand that “convenience” can increase energy use if devices run unnecessarily.
  • Green skills: Students evaluate the trade-offs between cleaning performance and energy use.
Time needed60 min
Resources needed Calculator, electricity tariff (e.g. €0.30/kWh), product specifications from manufacturer websites or appliance labels.

Introduction

Keeping floors clean is a daily or weekly household task. The choice of vacuum cleaner has changed dramatically — where once only corded upright or cylinder machines existed, robotic vacuums now navigate rooms autonomously. But which option uses more energy? The answer depends not only on the motor power but also on how often and how long the device runs. In this worksheet you will calculate and compare the true energy cost of floor care.

Theory

  • Traditional vacuum cleaners: use a motor of typically 700–2400 W for corded models. Since 2014, EU Regulation (EU) No 666/2013 has capped rated power at 900 W. Higher wattage does not necessarily mean better suction — filter condition and nozzle design matter more. A typical cleaning session lasts 30–60 minutes.
  • Robotic vacuums: use a much smaller motor, typically 20–100 W, but run for longer sessions (60–150 minutes) and often run daily rather than weekly. They also draw power when docked for charging (typically 3–25 W standby). Some models run at night.
  • Cleaning frequency matters enormously for energy totals. A traditional vacuum used once a week uses far less energy per year than a robotic vacuum running every day, even if its power is lower.

EU energy labels for vacuum cleaners show:

  • rated power (W),
  • annual energy use (kWh, based on 50 cleaning cycles per year),
  • dust re-emission class,
  • noise level (dB),
  • cleaning performance on carpet and hard floors.

Worked example

Traditional vacuum: 900 W, 45 min per session, once a week = 52 sessions/year.

  • Annual energy: 0.9 kW × 0.75 h × 52 = 35.1 kWh

Robotic vacuum: 35 W, runs 90 min/day, 7 days/week = 365 sessions/year. Standby: 8 W for 22.5 h/day.

  • Run energy: 0.035 kW × 1.5 h × 365 = 19.2 kWh
  • Standby energy: 0.008 kW × 22.5 h × 365 = 65.7 kWh
  • Robotic total: 84.9 kWh — much more than the traditional vacuum!

Student activity 2

Standby and vampire power

“Vampire power” or “phantom load” is electricity consumed by devices when not in active use — charging on a dock, waiting in standby, or displaying a status light.

●Calculate the annual standby energy of the robotic vacuum (8 W × 22.5 h/day × 365 days). What percentage of its total annual energy is this?
●A smart power strip could cut standby to zero. How much money per year would this save? Is it worth paying €15 for the strip?
●Research: find two other household devices with significant standby consumption. Estimate their annual standby energy.

Additional tasks — for electrical engineering students

These additional questions are aimed at students enrolled in electrical or electronic engineering programmes.

●Draw a block diagram of a traditional vacuum cleaner (major components); indicate typical power ratings of each component.
●Why does a vacuum cleaner have a low power factor (cos φ ≈ 0.7–0.8)? How does this affect the electricity bill?
●If a vacuum draws 900 W at 230 V with cos φ = 0.75, calculate the current (I = P/(V × cos φ)).
●A vacuum cleaner has 900 W electrical input but only 150 W of suction power (air watts). Calculate the efficiency. Where does the rest of the energy go?
●Draw a block diagram of a robot vacuum (major components); indicate typical power ratings.
●What type of motor does a robot vacuum use (brushed DC, BLDC) and why? Discuss BLDC advantages (efficiency, noise, lifetime) and disadvantages (cost, control electronics).
