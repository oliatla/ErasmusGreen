Worksheet 05

What is inside a light bulb?
Level: BASIC
Topic What is inside a light bulb?
Learning outcomes
  • Recognise the four main types of household light bulbs.
  • Identify the wattage and lifetime of common bulb types.
Time needed 60 min
Resources needed Bulb information table (in this worksheet); access to one or more real bulbs if available.

Introduction

Not all light bulbs are the same. Some get hot, some last for years, and some use much less electricity. In this worksheet you will look at four common types of bulbs and learn the basic differences between them.

Theory

Every light bulb does the same job: it turns electricity into light. But not all bulbs do that equally well. Some bulbs waste a lot of electricity by also turning it into heat. That is why an old-fashioned bulb feels hot after only a minute, while an LED stays cool.

There are four bulb types you will see most often at home and at school. They all use the same electrical socket but they work in completely different ways inside.

The four bulb types

  • INCANDESCENT bulbs (the oldest type) have a thin metal wire inside. When electricity flows through the wire it gets so hot that it glows white. Most of the energy actually becomes heat, not light. These bulbs are no longer sold in most EU countries.
  • HALOGEN bulbs are an improved version of the incandescent bulb. They contain a small amount of halogen gas which makes the wire last a bit longer and burn slightly brighter. Still mostly heat though.
  • COMPACT FLUORESCENT (CFL) bulbs use a folded glass tube filled with gas. Electricity makes the gas glow. They use much less electricity than incandescent bulbs but contain a tiny amount of mercury, so they need to be recycled carefully.
  • LED bulbs (Light Emitting Diode) use a small electronic component that produces light directly when electricity flows through it. They are the most efficient: they convert almost all the electricity into light, and very little into heat. They also last 25 times longer than an incandescent bulb.

Worked example — How do you read the bulb information table?

  1. Look at the row Lifetime. The number tells you how many hours the bulb works before it needs to be replaced.
  2. Example: an LED bulb has a lifetime of 25,000 hours. If you use it 5 hours per day, that is 25,000 ÷ 5 = 5,000 days, which is about 13 years.
  3. Look at the row Wattage. This tells you how much electricity the bulb uses each second it is on. A higher wattage means more electricity.
  4. Example: an incandescent bulb of 60 W uses 5 times more electricity than an LED of 12 W to give the same amount of light.

Key terms

TermDefinition
Bulb A light source that screws or clicks into a lamp.
Lifetime The number of hours a bulb works before it stops working.
Wattage (W) How much electricity the bulb uses.
Lumens (lm) How much light the bulb produces (more lumens = brighter).
LED Light Emitting Diode — the most efficient bulb type today.
