Worksheet 11

Cleaning & household maintenance — washing machines and dryers
Level: MEDIUM
Topic Washing machines and dryers
Learning outcomes
  • Technical knowledge: Students learn how washing machines and dryers work, consume energy/water, and the difference between vented and condenser dryers.
  • Mathematical skills: Students calculate the cost savings of using eco/wash programmes and compare dryer types.
  • Environmental awareness: Students understand how laundry habits affect carbon footprint and water scarcity.
  • Green skills: Students evaluate trade-offs between convenience, cost, and environmental impact, and compare standard vs. eco settings and vented vs. condenser dryers.
Time needed90 min
Resources needed Calculator, electricity tariff (e.g. €0.30/kWh), water tariff (e.g. €0.002/L), appliance datasheets.

Introduction

The average European household does 3–5 wash loads per week. In this worksheet you will investigate how much energy and water a washing machine uses on different programmes, compare types of tumble dryer, and calculate what you could save by choosing eco settings.

Theory

A washing machine is one of the biggest water and electricity users in a home. Most of the energy goes into heating water — not spinning the drum. Modern washing machines include eco modes that use less water and lower temperatures, though they often take longer to run.

Heating water to 40 °C or 60 °C uses far more energy than running the motor. An Eco programme at 20–30 °C uses up to 60 % less energy but runs longer. The trade-off: longer cycle time but lower cost and less fabric damage.

Clothes dryers

Clothes dryers are even more energy-intensive. Understanding the difference between vented and condenser dryers — and when to avoid using a dryer at all — can save money and reduce emissions.

  • Vented dryer: pulls in room air, heats it, blows humid air out through a hose (must be vented outside). High energy use (typical: 2.5–3.5 kWh per load).
  • Condenser dryer: no vent needed. Moisture is collected in a tank. Slightly more efficient (2.0–3.0 kWh per load) but slower.
  • Heat-pump dryer: most efficient (1.0–1.5 kWh per load) — uses a refrigerant cycle like a fridge. More expensive to buy but cheaper to run.

Water use: washing machines typically use 40–90 litres per cycle, depending on drum size and programme. Eco programmes tend to use less water because they spin more slowly and re-use rinse water more efficiently.

Key formula for water heating

$$ \text{Energy} = \frac{\text{litres} \times 4.18 \times \Delta T}{3600} \ \text{kWh} $$

Simplified: ~0.05 kWh per litre per 10 °C temperature rise.

Worked example

A cotton 60 °C programme uses 1.8 kWh and 60 L per wash. The eco 40 °C programme uses 0.9 kWh and 45 L. The household does 4 washes per week.

  • Annual energy difference: (1.8 − 0.9) × 4 × 52 = 187.2 kWh
  • Annual cost saving at €0.30/kWh: 187.2 × 0.30 = €56.16/year
  • Annual water saving: (60 − 45) × 4 × 52 = 3,120 L

Reflection

●When would a condenser dryer be preferred over a heat-pump dryer despite higher running costs? (Think about space, installation, upfront budget.)
●If every household in a city of 100,000 switched to an eco washing programme (saving 93.6 kWh/year), how many kWh would be saved city-wide? Convert to MWh and GWh.
